# Bible Study Worksheet

## Psalms 117

*Praise the Lord, All Ye Nations*

> O praise the Lord, all ye nations: praise him, all ye people

**KJV** · Standalone study handout

---

## 1 · The Word (KJV)

**1** *O praise the LORD, all ye nations: praise him, all ye people.*

**2** *For his merciful kindness is great toward us: and the truth of the LORD endureth for ever. Praise ye the LORD.*

---

## 2 · Context

The shortest chapter in the entire Bible, Psalm 117 compresses the whole logic of universal praise into two lines: a summons addressed to every nation, and a reason—God's merciful kindness and enduring truth—stated with nothing wasted. Henry treats its brevity as its own kind of argument: some truths need no elaboration, only proclamation, and this psalm's inclusion of 'all nations' anticipates the gospel's later reach to the whole world, a connection Paul makes explicitly in Romans 15:11.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1

The call to praise is addressed with deliberate breadth from its very first word: all nations, all people—not Israel exclusively, but every people everywhere, summoned to praise the same Lord.

### Verse 2

The reason is given as briefly as the summons: His merciful kindness is great toward us, and His truth endures forever. Two attributes—mercy and enduring truth—are offered as sufficient grounds for the praise of the entire world, closing with the familiar refrain: praise the Lord.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

Some truths about God require no lengthy argument, only clear proclamation, and this psalm's brevity is itself part of its message: the reach of God's mercy and truth to 'all nations' needs nothing more elaborate than a direct, universal summons. Its inclusion of every people anticipates a scope the gospel would later make explicit, that praise belongs to the whole world, not to one nation's private possession.

### Application for life today

Let this psalm's brevity challenge any instinct to over-complicate your own praise—sometimes the most honest response to God's mercy and truth is simply to state it plainly and move on to actually doing it. Consider the psalm's global address and ask who beyond your own immediate circle needs to hear this same simple summons from you. Take just these two verses and use them literally as a short prayer of praise today, noticing how little is required to begin.
