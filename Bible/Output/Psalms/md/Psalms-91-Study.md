## 2 · Context

Psalm 91 is an unattributed song of refuge, likely placed here deliberately after Psalm 90's sober meditation on mortality: having just faced how brief and fragile life is, the Psalter answers with a portrait of the believer safely hidden in God regardless of what threatens. Henry reads it as a psalm for anyone facing danger, plague, or fear in the night; MacArthur-style notes highlight that its promises are addressed specifically to the one who actually dwells with God, not to people in general, and that Satan's misuse of verses 11–12 in tempting Christ shows how easily this psalm's confidence can be twisted into presumption.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-2

The psalm opens with a conditional portrait: the one who dwells in the secret place of the Most High, who abides under the shadow of the Almighty, is the one who can say of the Lord, 'my refuge and my fortress, my God, in whom I trust.' Safety is tied to nearness, not distance from danger.

### Verse 3-8

A sweeping catalog of dangers follows—the snare of the fowler, deadly pestilence, terror by night, arrow by day, plague at noonday—all named specifically rather than vaguely, and all answered by God's covering. Thousands may fall on every side, yet the one sheltered under His wings only watches the reward of the wicked from a place of safety, not because evil does not exist, but because it does not have the final word over the one who trusts.

### Verse 9-13

Because the Lord has been made a habitation, no evil shall befall, no plague come near the dwelling. Angels are given charge to keep in all ways, to bear up in their hands, so that the foot need not even strike a stone. The lion, the adder, the young lion, and the dragon—every form of hidden or open threat—are pictured as trodden underfoot, not merely avoided.

### Verse 14-16

The psalm closes with God's own speech, confirming the whole preceding promise: because he has set his love upon me, I will deliver him; I will set him on high because he has known my name. He shall call, and I will answer; I will be with him in trouble, deliver and honor him, satisfy him with long life, and show him my salvation. The final word on safety belongs to God Himself, not merely to poetic assurance.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

True security is not the absence of danger but the presence of God—dwelling continually with Him is what places a person under a covering no threat, however varied or terrifying, can finally penetrate. The psalm's promises are addressed to the one who has actually made the Lord a dwelling place and set love and trust upon His name, not offered as a blanket guarantee against all hardship regardless of relationship to Him.

### Application for life today

When fear catalogs its threats—by night, by day, hidden or visible—bring that same specificity to God rather than letting vague anxiety multiply in the dark; name what actually frightens you and set it deliberately under His covering. Notice that these promises belong to the one who dwells with God continually, not occasionally; consider what it would mean to make nearness to Him a daily habitation rather than an emergency measure. Remember Christ's own resistance to Satan's twisting of this psalm in the wilderness: genuine trust rests in God's care, but never presumes on it to force His hand.
