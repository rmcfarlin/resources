## 2 · Context

Psalm 96 widens its summons to worship far beyond Israel, calling all the earth and all the nations to recognize the Lord's greatness and coming judgment. Its 'new song' language signals fresh cause for praise—likely tied to a specific act of salvation—while its vision of creation itself rejoicing anticipates final, universal justice. Henry reads it as a missionary psalm before missions had a name, insisting that God's glory is not a private possession of Israel but a message meant for every nation.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-3

The call to sing a new song is addressed to all the earth, not merely to Israel: bless His name, show forth His salvation daily, declare His glory among the heathen and His wonders among all people. Worship is framed from the outset as a message meant to travel outward.

### Verse 4-6

The reason for this universal summons is comparative: the Lord is great and greatly to be praised, to be feared above all gods, since the gods of the nations are mere idols while the Lord made the heavens. Honour, majesty, strength, and beauty are said to be found specifically in His sanctuary—true glory has an actual source, not a scattered guess.

### Verse 7-9

The nations themselves are addressed directly: give unto the Lord glory and strength, bring an offering, worship Him in the beauty of holiness, and let all the earth tremble before Him. Worship here is not passive spectating but active, costly response.

### Verse 10-13

The psalm climaxes in a declaration to be spoken among the heathen: the Lord reigns, the world is established and will not be moved, and He will judge the people righteously. Creation itself is summoned to rejoice—heavens, earth, sea, fields, and every tree of the wood—because the Lord comes to judge the earth with righteousness and truth. Judgment, remarkably, is presented as good news worth creation's own celebration.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

Praise for God's greatness is never meant to remain a private or national possession; it is a message with an intended global audience, urging every nation and even creation itself to recognize the Lord's reign. Far from being a grim threat, God's coming judgment is pictured as cause for the whole created order to rejoice, since righteous judgment is what finally sets everything right.

### Application for life today

Consider your own worship as having an intended audience beyond yourself—what would it mean to actively 'declare his glory among the heathen' (v. 3) rather than keep praise privately contained? When God's judgment feels like an uncomfortable topic, let this psalm's vision reframe it: righteous judgment is good news that even trees and seas are pictured celebrating (vv. 11–12). Bring an actual offering, not just words, in response to God's worth, as verse 8 calls for.
