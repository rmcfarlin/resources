## 1 · The Word (KJV)

---

## 2 · Context

Psalm 94 is a plea for justice against arrogant, violent oppressors who exploit the vulnerable while assuming God either does not see or will not act. Its turning point is theological rather than circumstantial: the psalmist reasons that the God who gave eyes and ears to humanity must Himself see and hear, and that discipline from Him, however painful, is proof of relationship rather than abandonment. Henry reads it as a corrective for anyone tempted to think unpunished wickedness means unnoticed wickedness; MacArthur-style notes highlight verse 14's assurance—God will not cast off His people—as the psalm's load-bearing promise.

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-3

The opening cry addresses God directly by a title rarely used so plainly—the God to whom vengeance belongs—asking Him to show Himself, to lift up Himself against the proud. The complaint is stated as a question: Lord, how long shall the wicked, specifically the wicked, continue to triumph?

### Verse 4-7

The oppressors are described in their own words and actions: they speak arrogantly, all who work iniquity boast, they break in pieces God's own people and afflict His heritage, they slay the widow and the stranger and murder the fatherless. Worse than the violence itself is their reasoning—they say the Lord shall not see, the God of Jacob will not even take notice.

### Verse 8-11

The psalmist answers that reasoning head-on, addressing the 'brutish' and foolish among the people directly: will the one who planted the ear not hear, the one who formed the eye not see? The one who instructs nations and teaches man knowledge surely knows the thoughts of man already—futile thoughts included.

### Verse 12-15

A turn toward blessing: happy is the one whom God chastens and teaches from His law, giving rest from days of adversity while a pit is being dug for the wicked. The Lord will not cast off His people nor forsake His inheritance; judgment will in time return to righteousness, and all the upright in heart will follow it.

### Verse 16-19

Personal testimony follows: who would have risen up for me against evildoers, had the Lord not been my help? When he said his foot was slipping, God's mercy held him up; in the multitude of anxious thoughts within him, God's comforts brought delight to his soul—inner turmoil met directly by inner comfort, not merely by outward rescue.

### Verse 20-23

The psalm closes with confidence that a throne of iniquity can have no real fellowship with God, however it frames its injustice as law. Though the righteous may be gathered against and condemned as innocent blood, the Lord is a defense, a rock of refuge, and will bring the wicked's own iniquity back upon them, cutting them off in it—justice delayed, but not denied.

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

Unpunished wickedness is not unnoticed wickedness; the same God who designed human eyes and ears to perceive certainly perceives every act of oppression and every self-justifying lie that assumes He does not. Discipline from God, far from signaling abandonment, is evidence of ongoing relationship, and His people can trust that a throne built on injustice has no lasting fellowship with Him, whatever temporary power it holds.

### Application for life today

When cruelty or exploitation seems to go unanswered, resist the quiet lie that God has not noticed; bring the specific injustice to Him as directly as this psalm does, naming both the wrong and your desire for Him to act. If you are currently under God's discipline, let verses 12–13 reframe it as evidence of relationship and teaching rather than rejection. In your own 'multitude of thoughts' (v. 19), notice where anxious rumination needs to be met not just by information but by God's actual comfort, sought deliberately in prayer.
