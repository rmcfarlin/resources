## 2 · Context

Psalm 107 opens Book V of the Psalter with a structured celebration of God's rescue across four distinct scenarios—wanderers lost in the wilderness, prisoners in darkness and irons, the sick brought near death by their own folly, and sailors caught in a violent storm—each following the same pattern: distress, cry to the Lord, deliverance, and a call to give thanks. Henry treats the repeated refrain as deliberately instructive, training the reader to recognize the same divine rescue pattern across radically different kinds of trouble.

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-3

The call to thanksgiving opens broadly: give thanks, for He is good, His mercy endures forever. The redeemed are pictured as gathered from every direction—east, west, north, and south—a single community formed out of scattered rescue.

### Verse 4-9

The first scenario: wanderers in the wilderness, finding no city to dwell in, hungry and thirsty, their soul fainting within them. They cried to the Lord in their trouble, and He delivered them from their distresses, leading them by a right way to a city of habitation. The refrain follows: let them praise the Lord for His goodness, for He satisfies the longing soul and fills the hungry with goodness.

### Verse 10-16

The second scenario: those sitting in darkness and the shadow of death, bound in affliction and iron because they rebelled against God's words and despised His counsel. Their hearts brought down through hard labor, they fell down with no one to help—until they cried to the Lord and were saved, brought out of darkness, their bands broken. The refrain repeats, now specific to their rescue: He has broken gates of brass and cut bars of iron in two.

### Verse 17-22

The third scenario: fools afflicted because of their own transgression and iniquity, their soul abhorring all food, drawing near the gates of death. Again they cried to the Lord, and He saved them, sending His word and healing them, delivering them from destruction. The refrain calls them specifically to sacrifice thanksgiving and declare His works with rejoicing—healing tied to grateful testimony, not silent relief.

### Verse 23-32

The fourth and most vivid scenario: those who go down to the sea in ships, witnessing God's works in the deep—He commands, and a stormy wind lifts up the waves, sailors mounting up to heaven and down to the depths, their soul melting because of trouble, reeling like drunken men, at their wits' end. They cried to the Lord, and He stilled the storm, calming the waves, bringing them to their desired haven glad because of the quiet. The refrain here expands to public testimony: let them exalt Him in the assembly of the people and praise Him in the company of the elders.

### Verse 33-38

The psalm turns from individual rescue to broader providence: God turns rivers into wilderness and fruitful land into barrenness because of the wickedness of those who dwell there, yet also turns wilderness into pools of water, settling the hungry there to build a city, sow fields, plant vineyards, and be blessed with great increase—judgment and provision both shown as expressions of the same sovereign hand.

### Verse 39-43

A final movement traces decline and restoration in national life: brought low through oppression, affliction, and sorrow, yet God pours contempt on princes and causes them to wander, while setting the poor on high from affliction and making families increase like a flock. The righteous see this and rejoice, while iniquity is silenced; the psalm closes by naming its whole purpose: whoever is wise will observe these things and understand the Lord's lovingkindness.

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

God's rescuing mercy is not limited to one kind of crisis; the same pattern of cry, deliverance, and required thanksgiving repeats across wilderness wandering, imprisonment, self-inflicted sickness, and mortal danger at sea, showing His care to be as varied as human trouble itself. The psalm's explicit purpose is that observing this pattern should produce wisdom—recognizing God's lovingkindness at work across every category of need, not only the ones that feel dignified or blameless.

### Application for life today

Identify which of the four scenarios—wandering without direction, bound by consequences, suffering from your own folly, or overwhelmed by circumstances beyond your control—most describes your current situation, and let this psalm's specific refrain shape a specific prayer of thanksgiving in response. Notice that the third scenario (vv. 17–22) explicitly includes self-inflicted trouble—if your own choices contributed to a hardship, this psalm does not exclude you from crying out and being heard. Practice the psalm's insistence on public, not merely private, thanksgiving (vv. 22, 32)—declare specific rescues to others, not only to God.
