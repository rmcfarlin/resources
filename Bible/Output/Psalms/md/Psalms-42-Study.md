# Bible Study Worksheet

## Psalms 42

*As the Hart Panteth*

> As the hart panteth after the water brooks

**KJV** · Standalone study handout

---

## 1 · The Word (KJV)

**1** *As the hart panteth after the water brooks, so panteth my soul after thee, O God.*

**2** *My soul thirsteth for God, for the living God: when shall I come and appear before God?*

**3** *My tears have been my meat day and night, while they continually say unto me, Where is thy God?*

**4** *When I remember these things, I pour out my soul in me: for I had gone with the multitude, I went with them to the house of God, with the voice of joy and praise, with a multitude that kept holyday.*

**5** *Why art thou cast down, O my soul? and why art thou disquieted in me? hope thou in God: for I shall yet praise him for the help of his countenance.*

**6** *O my God, my soul is cast down within me: therefore will I remember thee from the land of Jordan, and of the Hermonites, from the hill Mizar.*

**7** *Deep calleth unto deep at the noise of thy waterspouts: all thy waves and thy billows are gone over me.*

**8** *Yet the LORD will command his lovingkindness in the daytime, and in the night his song shall be with me, and my prayer unto the God of my life.*

**9** *I will say unto God my rock, Why hast thou forgotten me? why go I mourning because of the oppression of the enemy?*

**10** *As with a sword in my bones, mine enemies reproach me; while they say daily unto me, Where is thy God?*

**11** *Why art thou cast down, O my soul? and why art thou disquieted within me? hope thou in God: for I shall yet praise him, who is the health of my countenance, and my God.*

---

## 2 · Context

Opening Book II of the Psalter, Psalm 42 is the soul-thirsty lament of someone exiled from the sanctuary, remembering festive worship while surrounded by mockers taunting, “Where is thy God?” It alternates between honest despair and deliberate self-address—“Why art thou cast down, O my soul?”—a refrain repeated to steady faith rather than simply describe feeling. Paired with Psalm 43 as a single composition split for liturgical use, it models talking back to one’s own soul in faith instead of only listening to one’s emotions.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-2

As the hart pants after the water brooks, so the soul pants after God; it thirsts for the living God and longs to appear before Him.

### Verse 3-5

Tears become his food day and night while enemies taunt, “Where is thy God?” He remembers leading the worshiping multitude to the house of God with joy and praise, then answers himself with the refrain: “Why art thou cast down, O my soul?… hope thou in God: for I shall yet praise him.”

### Verse 6-8

His soul is cast down within him, yet he remembers God even from the land of Jordan and Hermon; deep calls unto deep at the noise of God’s waterspouts, and waves and billows have gone over him. Still, the Lord commands His lovingkindness in the daytime and gives a song in the night, a prayer to “the God of my life.”

### Verse 9-11

He calls God “my rock” and asks why he has seemingly been forgotten, why he goes mourning under the enemy’s oppression—enemies who reproach him “as with a sword” by constantly asking, “Where is thy God?” The refrain returns once more: hope in God, for I shall yet praise Him.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

Spiritual thirst and felt distance from God are not the same as unbelief—they can coexist with real, if strained, faith. Deliberately preaching hope to one’s own soul, even mid-lament, is a legitimate and necessary act of faith, not a denial of genuine sorrow.

### Application for life today

When you feel “cast down,” practice the psalm’s pattern of speaking directly to your own soul rather than simply absorbing whatever it currently feels. Identify a past season of worship or closeness to God (like verse 4) to remember deliberately in a dry season now. Notice that even “deep calling to deep” affliction (v. 7) does not cancel God’s commanded lovingkindness—look for the “song in the night” He is giving even now.
