## 2 · Context

A grand processional and battle hymn of David, likely composed for the ark's journey (2 Sam 6) or a national festival, tracing God's power from the Exodus and wilderness march to His enthronement in Zion and reign over all kingdoms. It is quoted in Ephesians 4:8 concerning Christ's ascension and gift-giving to the church. Henry treats it as the Old Testament's great song of God as Warrior-King who defends the weak and receives tribute from the nations; MacArthur-style notes highlight its use of ancient triumphal procession imagery culminating in worldwide praise.

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-6

The psalm opens as a battle cry (echoing Num 10:35) that quickly turns tender: "Let God arise, let his enemies be scattered... Sing unto God, sing praises to his name... A father of the fatherless, and a judge of the widows, is God... God setteth the solitary in families." The same God who scatters enemies champions the fatherless, widows, and lonely.

### Verse 7-10

"O God, when thou wentest forth before thy people... the earth shook... the heavens also dropped at the presence of God... thou, O God, hast prepared of thy goodness for the poor." The Exodus and Sinai theophany are recalled as proof of God's protective, providing presence marching ahead of His people.

### Verse 11-14

"The Lord gave the word: great was the company of those that published it. Kings of armies did flee apace... though ye have lien among the pots, yet shall ye be as the wings of a dove covered with silver." Victory is announced and celebrated even by those who seemed passive or lowly, transformed into glittering beauty.

### Verse 15-18

"The hill of God is as the hill of Bashan... the chariots of God are twenty thousand... Thou hast ascended on high, thou hast led captivity captive: thou hast received gifts for men." God's dwelling surpasses Bashan's great mountains; His ascension with captured spoils anticipates Christ's ascension and gift-giving (Eph 4:8).

### Verse 19-23

"Blessed be the Lord, who daily loadeth us with benefits, even the God of our salvation... God shall wound the head of his enemies." Ongoing, daily benefit-giving is coupled with certain judgment on persistent enemies—both mercy and justice belong to this King.

### Verse 24-27

"They have seen thy goings, O God... the singers went before, the players on instruments followed after... Bless ye God in the congregations... There is little Benjamin with their ruler, the princes of Judah... Zebulun, and... Naphtali." A vivid worship procession names specific tribes taking their place in unified national praise.

### Verse 28-31

"Strengthen, O God, that which thou hast wrought for us... kings shall bring presents unto thee... Princes shall come out of Egypt; Ethiopia shall soon stretch out her hands unto God." Foreign nations, even former oppressors, are pictured submitting tribute to God—an early vision of Gentile inclusion in worship.

### Verse 32-35

"Sing unto God, ye kingdoms of the earth... Ascribe ye strength unto God: his excellency is over Israel... The God of Israel is he that giveth strength and power unto his people. Blessed be God." The psalm closes with a global summons to praise the God whose strength both governs Israel and empowers His people for all that lies ahead.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

God is Warrior and Father at once—scattering enemies while adopting orphans, marching through wilderness history while daily loading His people with benefits, ascending in triumph while inviting even former oppressor-nations to bring tribute. His strength is not only historical memory but present and future empowerment.

### Application for life today

Let the same God who scatters enemies shape how you pray for justice, while also remembering He is "father of the fatherless… judge of the widows"—strength and tenderness belong together in His character and should in yours. Recall specific "goings" of God in your own history as fuel for present praise. Trust that God still "daily loadeth us with benefits" even when the biggest battles feel unresolved, and expect His strength to be given, not just admired, for what's ahead.
