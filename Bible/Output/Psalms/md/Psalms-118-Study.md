## 2 · Context

Psalm 118 closes the Egyptian Hallel with a processional thanksgiving for deliverance from surrounding enemies, moving from communal refrain to intensely personal testimony and on to the striking image of a rejected stone becoming the cornerstone. Its language is quoted directly by Jesus regarding His own rejection and vindication (Matthew 21:42), and by the crowds at His triumphal entry (Matthew 21:9); Henry reads the whole psalm as ultimately pointing beyond any single deliverance to Christ's own rejection and exaltation.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-4

The opening call to thanksgiving is issued in successive waves—to Israel, to the house of Aaron, to all who fear the Lord—each affirming the same refrain: His mercy endures forever. Repetition itself becomes an act of corporate testimony, drawing every part of the worshiping community into the same declaration.

### Verse 5-9

Personal testimony follows: calling on the Lord in distress and being answered, set in a large place, no longer afraid because the Lord is on his side. The conclusion drawn is blunt and comparative: better to trust in the Lord than to put confidence in man, better to trust in the Lord than in princes—human help measured directly against divine and found insufficient.

### Verse 10-14

Being surrounded by hostile nations is recalled vividly, described three times over as being compassed about, yet each time answered by the same confidence: in the name of the Lord I will destroy them. Though thrust at severely, on the verge of falling, the Lord helped, becoming strength, song, and salvation.

### Verse 15-18

Rejoicing and salvation are pictured filling the tabernacles of the righteous, the Lord's right hand doing valiantly and being exalted. Even severe chastening is acknowledged honestly—the Lord has chastened sorely—yet without being handed over to death, life itself preserved specifically in order to declare God's works.

### Verse 19-21

A request to enter follows: open the gates of righteousness, so I may go in and praise the Lord—this gate belonging specifically to the righteous. Personal praise closes the movement: I will praise You, for You have heard me and become my salvation.

### Verse 22-24

The psalm's most quoted image arrives: the stone the builders rejected has become the head of the corner—this is the Lord's doing, marvelous in our eyes. The refrain that gives the psalm its title follows directly: this is the day the Lord has made; we will rejoice and be glad in it.

### Verse 25-29

A final urgent plea for salvation and prosperity leads into blessing pronounced on the one who comes in the Lord's name, welcomed from the house of the Lord. Light shown, sacrifice bound to the altar's horns, personal praise renewed—the psalm closes exactly as it opened: give thanks to the Lord, for He is good, His mercy enduring forever.

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

Deliverance from real, surrounding hostility is celebrated here not as luck but as direct evidence that trusting the Lord outperforms any confidence placed in human strength or status, however severe the chastening endured along the way. The image of a rejected stone becoming the cornerstone reframes apparent failure or rejection as potentially the very means of God's marvelous, ultimately celebrated work—a pattern the New Testament identifies explicitly with Christ.

### Application for life today

Take verse 24 literally today, not as a generic sentiment but as a specific claim about this actual day—rehearse one concrete reason it can be received as made and given by the Lord. Notice verses 8–9's blunt comparison and examine where you are currently trusting 'man' or 'princes' more instinctively than you are trusting God in a present difficulty. Consider a place in your own life that felt like rejection or failure, and ask whether, as with the rejected stone, God might be doing something with it that is not yet visible.
