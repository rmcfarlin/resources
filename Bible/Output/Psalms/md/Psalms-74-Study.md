# Bible Study Worksheet

## Psalms 74

*The Desolate Sanctuary*

> O God, why hast thou cast us off for ever?

**KJV** · Standalone study handout

---

## 1 · The Word (KJV)

**1** *O God, why hast thou cast us off for ever? why doth thine anger smoke against the sheep of thy pasture?*

**2** *Remember thy congregation, which thou hast purchased of old; the rod of thine inheritance, which thou hast redeemed; this mount Zion, wherein thou hast dwelt.*

**3** *Lift up thy feet unto the perpetual desolations; even all that the enemy hath done wickedly in the sanctuary.*

**4** *Thine enemies roar in the midst of thy congregations; they set up their ensigns for signs.*

**5** *A man was famous according as he had lifted up axes upon the thick trees.*

**6** *But now they break down the carved work thereof at once with axes and hammers.*

**7** *They have cast fire into thy sanctuary, they have defiled by casting down the dwelling place of thy name to the ground.*

**8** *They said in their hearts, Let us destroy them together: they have burned up all the synagogues of God in the land.*

**9** *We see not our signs: there is no more any prophet: neither is there among us any that knoweth how long.*

**10** *O God, how long shall the adversary reproach? shall the enemy blaspheme thy name for ever?*

**11** *Why withdrawest thou thy hand, even thy right hand? pluck it out of thy bosom.*

**12** *For God is my King of old, working salvation in the midst of the earth.*

**13** *Thou didst divide the sea by thy strength: thou brakest the heads of the dragons in the waters.*

**14** *Thou brakest the heads of leviathan in pieces, and gavest him to be meat to the people inhabiting the wilderness.*

**15** *Thou didst cleave the fountain and the flood: thou driedst up mighty rivers.*

**16** *The day is thine, the night also is thine: thou hast prepared the light and the sun.*

**17** *Thou hast set all the borders of the earth: thou hast made summer and winter.*

**18** *Remember this, that the enemy hath reproached, O LORD, and that the foolish people have blasphemed thy name.*

**19** *O deliver not the soul of thy turtledove unto the multitude of the wicked: forget not the congregation of thy poor for ever.*

**20** *Have respect unto the covenant: for the dark places of the earth are full of the habitations of cruelty.*

**21** *O let not the oppressed return ashamed: let the poor and needy praise thy name.*

**22** *Arise, O God, plead thine own cause: remember how the foolish man reproacheth thee daily.*

**23** *Forget not the voice of thine enemies: the tumult of those that rise up against thee increaseth continually.*

---

## 2 · Context

Asaph writes after catastrophe: the sanctuary has been ransacked, its carved work hacked down, its buildings burned, and no prophetic word breaks the silence. This is communal lament at its rawest, likely composed in view of an invasion (whether Babylonian or an earlier crisis) that left worship itself desecrated. Henry notes the psalm's pivot from present ruin to remembered power—recalling the exodus and creation to relearn what kind of God has, seemingly, gone quiet.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-3

The opening cry names the crisis theologically before describing it physically: why has God cast off His own congregation, the flock He purchased and redeemed long ago? The plea is for God to remember Zion, His dwelling place, now trampled into perpetual ruin.

### Verse 4-8

The desecration is described in painful detail: enemies roar within the very congregation, set up their own banners where God's people once worshiped, hack down carved work with axes, and burn the sanctuary to the ground, declaring their intent to wipe out every trace of God's presence in the land.

### Verse 9-11

The deepest wound is not only physical but spiritual: there is no longer a prophet, no one who knows how long this will last. The question 'why do you hold back your hand' captures the disorientation of a people who cannot even get a word from God about their own disaster.

### Verse 12-17

In the middle of ruin, the psalmist deliberately rehearses old victories: God as King of old working salvation, dividing the sea, breaking the heads of sea monsters, splitting rock to bring forth water, establishing day and night, sun and boundaries, summer and winter. Creation and exodus become evidence that this God's power has not diminished, whatever the present silence suggests.

### Verse 18-21

The plea returns to the immediate need: remember how the enemy has mocked, how a foolish people has blasphemed your name. Do not deliver the life of the afflicted—likened to a helpless dove—into permanent oppression; let the poor and needy who still praise your name not be put to lasting shame.

### Verse 22-23

The psalm ends unresolved, mid-cry: arise, O God, plead your own cause, remember how the enemy reproaches you all day long, and do not forget the ever-rising noise of those who oppose you. There is no tidy resolution—only a final, urgent appeal left open before God.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

Communal disaster, especially when worship itself is desecrated, raises the hardest question believers can ask: has God abandoned His own people and His own house? The psalm's answer is not an explanation but a discipline—deliberately remembering what God has done in creation and redemption as the basis for continuing to appeal to Him, even when the present offers no evidence and no prophetic word.

### Application for life today

When a season of crisis leaves you without any clear sense of God's presence or plan, let this psalm model naming the loss specifically to God rather than only processing it privately. Practice the psalmist's discipline of deliberately rehearsing what God has done in the past—in Scripture and in your own life—as an anchor when the present offers no comfort. Recognize that ending a prayer unresolved, mid-appeal, is itself a legitimate form of faith.
