## 2 · Context

Psalm 119 is by far the longest chapter in the Bible, an elaborate acrostic of twenty-two eight-verse sections, one for each letter of the Hebrew alphabet, in which nearly every line names some aspect of God's word—law, testimonies, statutes, commandments, judgments, precepts, word, ways. Henry reads its sheer length and structure as deliberate: a whole alphabet devoted to a single subject, modeling exhaustive, unhurried meditation rather than a quick devotional glance. MacArthur-style notes highlight that the psalm was likely composed during real affliction (its author repeatedly mentions persecution, mockery, and near-death trouble), so that its praise of God's word is not naive but forged specifically under pressure.

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-8

Aleph. The psalm opens by naming blessedness for those whose way is blameless, who walk in the Lord's law and keep His testimonies, seeking Him with the whole heart rather than half-attention. A personal request follows almost immediately—that God would direct the psalmist's own ways to keep His statutes—admitting the desire is genuine but not yet fully realized.

### Verse 9-16

Beth. A young man's need to cleanse his way is answered directly: by taking heed according to God's word, sought with the whole heart to avoid wandering from His commandments. The word is hidden in the heart specifically to prevent sin, and the psalmist commits to rehearsing God's statutes with his lips and delighting in His testimonies as in great riches.

### Verse 17-24

Gimel. A plea to deal bountifully so that the psalmist may live and keep God's word is paired with a request to open his eyes to see wondrous things out of the law. Confessing himself a stranger and sojourner on earth, he asks that commandments not be hidden from him, even while princes sit and speak against him—persecution met by continued meditation on God's statutes as his counselors.

### Verse 25-32

Daleth. The psalmist's soul cleaves to the dust, and he asks to be quickened according to God's word; heaviness of soul is met by a request for strengthening according to that same word. Having chosen the way of truth and set God's judgments before him, he asks not to be put to shame, promising to run in the way of God's commandments once his heart is enlarged.

### Verse 33-40

He. Four requests for instruction open the section: teach me, give me understanding, make me go in the path of Your commandments, incline my heart to Your testimonies rather than to covetousness. Turning away his eyes from vanity and establishing God's word are both asked for, along with removal of reproach and rebuke, since he has hoped in God's judgments.

### Verse 41-48

Vav. Mercy and salvation according to God's word are asked for specifically so that he might have an answer for those who reproach him, trusting in that word. He resolves to keep God's law continually, speak of His testimonies before kings without shame, and delight himself in commandments he loves, lifting up his hands toward them.

### Verse 49-56

Zayin. God's word to His servant is asked to be remembered, since it has been his hope and comfort in affliction, even while the proud mock him excessively without turning him from God's law. Remembering God's judgments of old comforts him, even as indignation rises against those who have forsaken the law, and God's statutes become songs in his time of sojourning.

### Verse 57-64

Heth. The Lord is named directly as his portion, prompting a promise to keep His words and to seek His favor with his whole heart. Considering his own ways, he turns his feet to God's testimonies, making haste rather than delaying to keep His commandments, and asks to be a companion of all who fear the Lord and keep His precepts.

### Verse 65-72

Teth. God's good dealing with His servant is affirmed, followed by a request for good judgment and knowledge because he has believed God's commandments. A striking admission follows: before he was afflicted he went astray, but now he keeps God's word, affirming that the affliction itself has been good for him, teaching statutes better than the law of gold and silver could.

### Verse 73-80

Yod. God's hands having made and fashioned him grounds a request for understanding to learn His commandments; those who fear God are asked to see him and be glad, because he has hoped in God's word. Even in affliction, acknowledged as deserved and faithful correction, he asks for tender mercies and that his heart be sound in God's statutes so he is not put to shame.

### Verse 81-88

Kaph. His soul faints for God's salvation, his eyes fail waiting for God's word, and he asks how long until judgment is executed on his persecutors—weariness and honest impatience voiced directly. Nearly consumed on earth by those who have dug pits for him, he affirms he has not forsaken God's precepts, asking to be quickened according to God's lovingkindness.

### Verse 89-96

Lamed. God's word is declared settled forever in heaven, His faithfulness reaching every generation, His ordinances establishing the earth itself which still stands as His servants. Had God's law not been his delight, he confesses he would have perished in his affliction; he will never forget precepts that have given him life.

### Verse 97-104

Mem. Love for God's law is declared directly—meditated on all day—making him wiser than his enemies, giving more understanding than his teachers and the aged, because he keeps God's testimonies and precepts. Restraining his feet from every evil way in order to keep God's word, he finds God's judgments so sweet that he hates every false way.

### Verse 105-112

Nun. God's word is called a lamp to his feet and light to his path, and he has sworn, with sworn resolve kept, to keep God's righteous judgments. Greatly afflicted, taking his life continually in his hand amid snares set by the wicked, he does not stray from God's precepts, having taken His testimonies as a heritage forever, his heart set to perform God's statutes to the very end.

### Verse 113-120

Samekh. Vain thoughts are hated, but God's law is loved; God is named a hiding place and shield, and hope is placed specifically in His word. The wicked are asked to depart, and God is asked to hold him up and uphold him, while all who stray from His statutes, however numerous, are set aside like dross, God's testimonies still loved despite his own awe at God's judgments.

### Verse 121-128

Ayin. Having done judgment and justice, he asks not to be left to his oppressors, pleading for surety on his own behalf against the proud. His eyes fail waiting for God's salvation and righteous word, asking to be dealt with according to mercy and taught God's statutes, since it is time for the Lord to act when others have made His law void—prompting a love for God's commandments above gold and fine gold.

### Verse 129-136

Pe. God's testimonies are called wonderful, and the entrance of His words gives light, giving understanding even to the simple. Panting with longing for God's commandments, he asks his steps be ordered by God's word and that no iniquity have dominion over him, his eyes running with rivers of tears because others do not keep God's law.

### Verse 137-144

Tsadhe. God is declared righteous and His judgments upright, His testimonies very righteous and faithful; zeal consumes him because his enemies have forgotten God's words. Though small and despised, and though trouble and anguish have taken hold, God's commandments remain his delights, everlasting righteousness he asks understanding of in order to live.

### Verse 145-152

Qoph. Crying with his whole heart, he asks to be heard and saved, keeping God's testimonies; rising before dawn and crying for help, he hopes in God's word, his eyes anticipating the night watches to meditate on it. Those drawing near with mischief are far from God's law, yet the Lord is near, His testimonies founded of old and forever.

### Verse 153-160

Resh. Affliction is brought before God with a plea for deliverance, since he has not forgotten God's law; his cause is pleaded and he asks to be quickened according to God's word. Salvation, he observes, is far from the wicked because they do not seek God's statutes, while God's tender mercies are great and His word true from the very beginning.

### Verse 161-168

Shin. Princes persecute him without cause, yet his heart stands in awe of God's word rather than of them, rejoicing in it like someone who has found great treasure. Hating and abhorring lying, he loves God's law, praising Him seven times a day for righteous judgments, with great peace belonging to those who love that law and nothing able to offend them.

### Verse 169-176

Taw. A final concentrated plea closes the psalm: let his cry come near, give him understanding, let his supplication come before God, deliver him according to God's word. His lips are asked to pour forth praise once taught God's statutes, his tongue to speak of God's word, his hand held out for help—closing with a strikingly humble admission that he has gone astray like a lost sheep, asking God to seek His servant, since he has not forgotten God's commandments.

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

An entire alphabet devoted to a single subject models what unhurried, exhaustive meditation on God's word actually looks like—not a quick glance for comfort, but sustained attention that returns to the same truths from every angle, under real affliction, persecution, and honest impatience. The psalmist's love for God's word is forged specifically under pressure, not despite it, and the psalm's own closing image—a sheep gone astray, still asking to be sought—refuses to let all this devotion curdle into self-sufficient pride.

### Application for life today

Choose one section of this psalm and actually practice its acrostic discipline: spend unhurried time meditating on a single truth from God's word today rather than moving quickly past it. Notice how often affliction and persecution appear alongside declarations of love for God's word (vv. 71, 75, 92, 143, 153)—let that reshape any assumption that hardship and delight in Scripture are incompatible. Take the psalm's final verse personally: however much devotion you have practiced, come to God still as a sheep gone astray asking to be sought, not as someone who has arrived.
