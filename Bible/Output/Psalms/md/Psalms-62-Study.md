*Wait Thou Only Upon God*

> My soul, wait thou only upon God

---

## 2 · Context

A meditation of David on exclusive trust amid enemies who plot to topple him ("a bowing wall… a tottering fence"), likely from a season of political instability. Henry stresses the repeated word "only"—God alone as rock, salvation, and refuge—as the psalm's central discipline against divided trust.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-4

"Truly my soul waiteth upon God: from him cometh my salvation. He only is my rock and my salvation." Enemies seek to destroy him "as a bowing wall," delighting in lies and blessing with the mouth while cursing inwardly—instability all around, met by settled trust.

### Verse 5-8

He preaches to his own soul: "My soul, wait thou only upon God... He is my rock... Trust in him at all times; ye people, pour out your heart before him." Public exhortation follows private discipline—trust is meant to be modeled, not only privately held.

### Verse 9-12

"Men of low degree are vanity, and men of high degree are a lie... trust not in oppression... if riches increase, set not your heart upon them." Power belongs to God, and to Him alone belongs mercy—closing with the double truth heard "twice," that strength and steadfast love both come from Him.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

The word "only" is the psalm's hinge: salvation, rock, and trust must rest exclusively in God—not divided among human status, oppression's advantage, or increasing riches—because power and mercy both belong solely to Him.

### Application for life today

Notice where you quietly split trust between God and something else and practice saying "he only" specifically about that thing. Preach truth to your own soul as David does (v. 5), rather than waiting for feelings to catch up. When riches or influence increase, deliberately "set not your heart upon them," redirecting gratitude back to God's power and mercy.
