---

## 2 · Context

Superscribed to the time David feigned madness before Achish (1 Samuel 21), Psalm 34 is a Hebrew acrostic of praise and testimony after narrow deliverance. David invites others to “taste and see” God’s goodness for themselves, teaches the fear of the Lord as the path to good days, and promises nearness to the brokenhearted. The New Testament draws on it repeatedly (1 Peter 3:10–12; John 19:36 on verse 20), showing its lasting place in Christian devotion.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-3

A vow to bless the Lord “at all times” and to boast in Him rather than in circumstances; an invitation to magnify the Lord together with others who share the testimony.

### Verse 4-7

He sought the Lord and was delivered from his fears; those who “look unto him” are lightened, unashamed. The poor man cried and was saved out of all his troubles, and the angel of the Lord encamps round about those who fear Him.

### Verse 8-10

The famous invitation: “O taste and see that the Lord is good.” Blessed is the one who trusts Him; those who fear the Lord shall lack nothing good, even while young lions may go hungry.

### Verse 11-14

A teaching section addressed to “children”—come, hear, and be taught the fear of the Lord: keep the tongue from evil, depart from evil and do good, seek peace and pursue it.

### Verse 15-18

The eyes of the Lord are on the righteous, His ears open to their cry, but His face is against evildoers. He hears the righteous and delivers them out of all their troubles; He is near to the brokenhearted and saves the contrite in spirit.

### Verse 19-22

Many are the afflictions of the righteous, but the Lord delivers out of them all and keeps all his bones (fulfilled at the cross, John 19:36). Evil slays the wicked in the end, but the Lord redeems the soul of His servants; none who trust in Him shall be desolate.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

Goodness tasted firsthand is more persuasive than goodness merely described—David invites experiential trust, not blind assent. The fear of the Lord produces both practical wisdom (guarded speech, pursued peace) and real nearness to the brokenhearted, even though affliction is not promised away.

### Application for life today

Rather than only reading about God’s goodness, actively “taste” it this week—notice specific answered prayers, provisions, or comforts and name them as His goodness. If you are brokenhearted or crushed in spirit right now, verse 18 promises God is near, not distant, in exactly that condition. Practice verses 13–14 concretely: guard one habit of speech and pursue peace in one strained relationship.
