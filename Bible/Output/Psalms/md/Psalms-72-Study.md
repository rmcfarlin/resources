## 2 · Context

Superscribed for Solomon, Psalm 72 reads on the surface level as David's prayer for his son's coronation—justice for the poor, prosperity for the land, dominion to the ends of the earth. But the language quickly outgrows any merely human king: universal reign, endless duration, blessing extended to all nations. Henry and the broader Messianic tradition read it as reaching toward a greater Son of David; MacArthur-style notes treat Solomon's best moments as a shadow of Christ's perfect and everlasting kingship.

---

## 3 · Verse-by-Verse Commentary

*The notes below cover the whole chapter in one place—read with the KJV text above.*

### Verse 1-4

The prayer opens with a request for righteous judgment given to the king, so that he may judge the people with justice and the poor with equity. The measure of good kingship here is not military glory but how the vulnerable are treated—mountains bringing peace, the poor being defended, the oppressor broken.

### Verse 5-11

The king's reign is pictured in cosmic and universal terms: enduring as long as sun and moon, coming down like rain on the earth, dominion from sea to sea and to the ends of the world. Even rival kings and desert tribes are said to bow before him and bring tribute—language too large for any single historical monarch, straining toward the Messiah.

### Verse 12-17

The center of the psalm turns to compassion: he delivers the needy when they cry, has pity on the poor, redeems their life from oppression and violence because their blood is precious to him. Prosperity for the land follows just rule; his name is prayed to endure forever, with all nations calling him blessed.

### Verse 18-20

A closing doxology to the Lord God of Israel—who alone does wondrous things—caps the psalm, and the note 'the prayers of David are ended' marks a formal close to this earlier collection. The king's glory is finally swallowed up in the glory of God, whose praise fills the whole earth.

---

## 4 · Meaning & Application

### Meaning

True kingship, as this psalm defines it, is measured by justice for the poor and protection for the oppressed, not by conquest for its own sake. The language deliberately overflows any single ruler's lifetime, pointing forward to a King whose name and reign never end—one in whom, ultimately, all nations are blessed.

### Application for life today

Use this psalm to evaluate leadership—your own influence, or those you pray for—by its fruit for the vulnerable, not merely its scale or success. Let the psalm's global horizon expand your prayers beyond your own concerns toward the nations. And read the extravagant language as an invitation to hope in Christ's reign now, even while earthly kingdoms remain partial and passing.
